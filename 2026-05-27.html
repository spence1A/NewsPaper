Privacy May 8 · CCPA

GM Secretly Sold Your Driving Data. California Just Charged a Record Price.

California's Attorney General announced a record $12.75 million settlement with General Motors on May 8th — the largest CCPA penalty in history. From 2020 through 2024, GM's OnStar service collected names, precise GPS location, and granular driving behavior data (speed, hard braking, rapid acceleration) from hundreds of thousands of California drivers and sold it to LexisNexis Risk Solutions and Verisk — generating roughly $20 million in revenue — without meaningful notice or consent. GM must now stop selling this data to consumer reporting agencies for five years.

280 Million School Records Stolen in Massive Canvas Platform Hack

A breach of the Canvas learning management platform exposed nearly 280 million records belonging to faculty, staff, and students across 8,809 colleges, online learning platforms, and school districts. A New York City healthcare system disclosed one of 2026's most serious data breaches: hackers accessed personal and medical data for at least 1.8 million people, including biometric scans such as fingerprints. AI Policy Legislation

Colorado Just Pumped the Brakes on Its Own AI Law

Colorado Governor Jared Polis signed SB 189 into law, revising the state's landmark AI regulation and pushing its effective date from June 30, 2026, to January 1, 2027. Colorado's AI Act was the first broad US state law attempting to regulate high-risk AI systems — and the delay signals just how much pressure legislators face from the tech industry and from companies struggling to meet compliance deadlines. The amendment also modifies key provisions, giving businesses more runway but raising questions about whether the final law will have real teeth when it eventually takes effect.

Scientists Just Made Matter That Shouldn't Exist

Physicists published findings this month describing the creation of exotic new forms of matter that the standard model of physics suggested couldn't exist under ordinary conditions. The work, emerging from quantum materials research, demonstrated that under precisely engineered conditions — using a technique called Flux-Switching Floquet Engineering — matter can be forced into configurations that defy normal thermodynamic behavior. Separately, researchers also documented that ordinary liquids can snap apart like solid objects under the right circumstances, upending another long-held assumption about the physics of fluids.
